After we completed our interviews with the White Horse Farm, we tried to view the issue of the transformation of rural farming industries from a different angle, which is why, after Chinese New Year, we interviewed the division chief, Mr. Yeh, of the Agricultural Division in the Hsienhsi Township Office, and asked him questions regarding the issue of transformation of rural and agricultural industries …

Our team is currently working on a research project on the local “White Horse Farm” in Hsienhsi, which has experienced transformations from duck raising to horse farms, and then to wineries, and now to the integrated operation of a recreational tourism farm. This background seems similar to many of the farms in various locations of Taiwan that are seeking transformation. What types of bottlenecks do you think Taiwan’s traditional farming industries are facing right now?

In reality, the common problem our traditional farming industries are facing right now is the aging of the population. In addition, the government’s long-term policy for planning of farming land has resulted in small farming land areas, making it difficult for farmers to effectively engage in farming on larger land areas. Regarding the Hsienhsi Township, its geographic location is situated close to the western shoreline, making the weather here in winter relatively cold and less suitable for agricultural activities. Young people find it's difficult to make a living here, and therefore a large number of them move to other areas to find work, which has further caused the area’s farming population to age. These are some of the reasons for the issues we are currently facing in the area.
Which are the best agricultural products produced here in Hsienhsi?

Hsienhsi’s agricultural products are relatively simple. Our crops mostly consist of rice crops, while vegetables are mostly grown during slack time before planting. Therefore, our best agricultural crops are rice crops. We also have a rather high number of farmers that produce quail; therefore, we also have a good production of quail eggs and processed quail egg manufactures such as preserved quail eggs. In terms of animal husbandry, our local chicken industry is also quite developed.
Regarding our rice crops, because our farms have relatively smaller areas, we do not produce as much rice in terms of quantities as other areas. However, in terms of rice-producing techniques, the quality and production quantity per unit area of our first rice crop is rather good, but the second crop starts to be affected by the weather and is not as good as the first. Generally speaking, the overall rice crops produced in this area are not bad.

Which portions of Hsienhsi’s farming industries have been on the decline and have fallen?

For the agriculture industry, sweet potatoes used to be produced here, but no more. For the animal husbandry industry, duck raising used to be popular, but the industry is also no longer found here. Because of pollution from dioxin, the water and soil have been polluted, making it impossible for ducks to survive.
At present, the duck raisers that were affected by dioxin pollution are receiving subsidies from the government, and have therefore ceased their duck-raising activities. The land currently cannot be used for other purposes, and we must wait until the lifting of the land usage ban in 2014, and then see whether we want to once again use the land for duck raising or if we want to use the land for other purposes. Currently, Hsienhsi only has two farms producing duck eggs, but their products must be tested before they can be cleared for sale on the market.
Have the long-term farmers in the area been able to successfully cope with the current farming economy, or have they been able to further transform into other types of economic activities?

The farmers here are mostly relatively old-aged, and less willing to change the crop types that they grow. Most of the people in our area grow rice crops, only one person grows pears. As you can see, only a very small percentage of people grow other crops. There are in fact people that want to make changes, but they do not know what else to grow. In addition, locals are also afraid that there may not be a market for them to sell the other types of crops that they grow. Our previous experience with growing sweet potatoes proved to be a failure because we could not find the right channels or the right market to sell our products. I think although local farmers do want to change, they are also afraid that they may once again run into similar problems. This is why the sentiment for change is not high around here. Unless there are young people who are willing to come home and engage in farming activities, in that case, we may have a chance of adding some new ideas and new ways of thinking.
Has the farming industry transformation program in recent years had any positive effects? What obstacles are currently present?

Our township government is actually cooperating with many of the Council of Agriculture’s programs and policies, such as the revitalization and reuse of unused farmland. Because of Taiwan’s participation in the WTO, the government has opened up Taiwan to import rice products, and therefore the demand for domestic rice has naturally declined. This is why the government has been encouraging people to grow less rice crops and more of other crop types.
We have in the past tried to promote recreational farming in the entire western Hsienhsi area, including DingChuang, Hsienhsi, Yupu, Wenchai, and Gounei, but due to our limited resources, limited history, and limited cultural resources, these efforts were not successful, and we were not able to form a scenic route or scenic area.
All over Taiwan, farm recreation has become very popular. Does Hsienhsi have any other industries that might be able to take advantage of this trend?

The recreational farming facilities in Hsienhsi are still relatively small-scale and in there early stages of development. An example of such a relatively small-scale and early-stage farming facility would be the White Horse Farm. The soft and hard infrastructural facilities in the area have not yet been developed. For example, we do not have any hotel accommodation facilities for providing visitors with lodging services, and therefore tourists who come here can only stay for a short while, because there is nowhere for them to stay for the night. We currently do not have enough resources to develop a recreational farming industry; we do not have the ability to establish a full set of supportive services. Our county government administration office holds one relatively large promotional activity per year, which is an activity similar to the Mullet Festival, and hopes to use that promotion to present the specialty products of Hsienhsi to the rest of the country.
What additional efforts does Hsienhsi have to make to be able to successfully transform its traditional farming industry into a tourism and recreational farming industry?

In fact, Hsienhsi can successfully transform into a tourism and recreational services economy not only because of the efforts from government organizations, but also the help and cooperation of the local community and environment as a whole. We need our residents and citizens to unite and collaboratively work towards this goal. Take, for example, the farming village transformation program that the government is currently promoting encourages- each neighborhood and each community to create its own specialty and unique characteristics. Each neighborhood will have its own unique selling point, making each and every house a unique site for tourists to see and visit. With a program like this, as long as we continually develop such a concept, people from other areas of the country and even the world will slowly begin to develop interest in our town and come to visit. Once this happens, the next thing we need to do is to build our infrastructure – we need to build hotels and lodging facilities so that visitors can stay longer, and be able to treat Hsienhsi as the key destination of their vacation rather than just a point on the map to temporarily stop at. Once we connect all of these dots together, we will have succeeded in transforming our town into a successful and attracting recreational farming location.

The current rural farming industry transformation and revitalization programs take a top-down approach. This is why it is important for the residents of each neighborhood to be spontaneous in realizing many of the concepts of recreational farming and tourism. Residents must complete the farming land revitalization courses provided by the government, because only then will they have the knowledge and momentum to engage in planning of their businesses; only then will they be able to engage in long-term maintenance, operation, and management of their recreational farming facilities; only then will we be able to write strong and convincing business plans to request funding from the central government to realize and build the facilities and infrastructure that we need to achieve our goals. As you can see, we cannot just blindly write business plans and ask for funding, the spontaneous and active participation of all local residents is the real key to successful transformation.
